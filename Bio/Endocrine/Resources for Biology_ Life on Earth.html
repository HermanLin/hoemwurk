Audesirk / Audesirk Biology: Life on Earth Chapter 35 Chemical Control of the Animal Body: The Endocrine System

Lecture Outline

I. The Endocrine System

A. Hormones and Other Signaling Molecules

1. Signaling molecules are hormones and secretions that can bind to target cells and elicit a response.

2. There are four main types of signaling molecules:

a. Hormones are secreted from endocrine sources and some neurons and are then transported by the blood to remote targets.

b. Neurotransmitters are secreted from neurons and act on immediately adjacent target cells for a short time.

c. Local signaling molecules are secreted from cells of many different tissues; they act locally and are swiftly degraded.

d. Pheromones, which are secreted by exocrine glands, have targets outside the body; they integrate social activities between animals.

B. Discovery of Hormones and Their Sources

1. In the early 1900s, Bayliss and Starling first demonstrated that a hormone (later named secretin) released into the blood triggers secretion of pancreatic juices.

2. Starling coined the word hormone for internal secretions released into the bloodstream that influence the activities of other tissues and organs.

3. The sources of hormones may be collectively called the endocrine system, which is intimately connected to the nervous system.

C. There are four main classes of chemicals used as hormones:

1. Peptide hormones are composed of chains of amino acids. i.e. insulin, oxytocin, TRH.

2. A few hormones are modified amino acids. For example, the amino acid tyrosine is the structural basis for epinephrine (adrenaline), norepinephrine (noradrenaline), and thyroxine.

3. Steroid hormones are composed of cholesterol. Testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, aldosterone, and cortisol are steroid hormones.

4. Prostaglandins are composed of two fatty acid chains attached to a five carbon ring.

D. Modes of Action of Hormones: Signaling Mechanisms Figure 35-2

1. A target cell's response to a hormone is dependent on two factors:

a. Different hormones activate different cellular response mechanisms.

b. Not all cells have receptors for all hormones; the cells that respond are selected by means of the type of receptor they possess.

2. Steroid hormones, assembled from cholesterol, are lipid&endash;soluble and therefore cross plasma membranes readily.

a. Steroids stimulate or inhibit protein (especially enzyme) synthesis by switching certain genes on or off.

b. They easily diffuse through the lipid bilayer of the plasma membrane, bind to chromosomal proteins in the nucleus, and then activate transcription.

c. Testosterone is a male hormone, with receptors throughout the body; however, in testicular feminization syndrome, none of the target cells respond correctly, so the XY individual develops female characteristics.

3. Nonsteroid hormones include amines, amino acids, peptides, proteins, and glycoproteins.

a. Protein hormones and other water&endash;soluble signaling molecules cannot cross the plasma membrane of target cells so they must first bind to a receptor on the plasma membrane.

b. The hormone&endash;receptor complex stimulates the production of cyclic AMP, a "second messenger" that amplifies the signal by activating numerous enzymes.

E. Selected Examples of Hormonal Control

1. When considering the actions of hormones, three points are relevant:

a. Hormones interact to oppose (antagonistic), add to (synergistic), or prime (permissive interaction) target cells for another hormone's effects.

b. Many hormones are linked to the neural&endash;endocrine control center by homeostatic feedback loops.

c. The response of target cells depends on the number of their receptors and the concentration of the hormone.

2. Consult Table 35-2 for hormone source, secretion(s), and primary actions.

F. Hormones are Regulated by Feedback Mechanisms

1. Negative feedback is an important way of maintaining homeostasis within the body. During negative feedback, the secretion of a hormone causes effects in target cells that inhibit further secretion of the hormone.

a. In response to loss of water from your bloodstream, ADH causes your kidneys to reabsorb more water and produce a very concentrated urine. Negative feedback ensures that when your blood water content returns to normal, ADH secretion is turned off and your kidneys begin eliminating the excess water (see Fig 33-8).

2. In a few cases, positive feedback controls hormone release. Uterus contractions during childbirth cause the release of oxytocin. Oxytocin stimulates stringer contractions of the uterus which causes more oxytocin to be released creating a positive feedback cycle.

F. Endocrine and Exocrine Glands Figure 35-1

1. Glands are single cells or multicelled secretory structures.

2. Exocrine glands often secrete through ducts to free surfaces; they secrete mucus, saliva, wax, milk, and so on outside the body or into the digestive tract..

3. Endocrine glands secrete hormones directly into intercellular fluid for distribution by the blood.

II. The Hypothalamus and the Pituitary Gland

A. The hypothalamus and pituitary gland work jointly as the neural&endash;endocrine control center.

1. The hypothalamus is a portion of the brain that monitors internal conditions and emotional states.

2. The pituitary is a pea&endash;sized gland connected to the hypothalamus by a stalk.

a. The posterior lobe of the pituitary consists of nervous tissue and releases two neurohormones made in the hypothalamus.

b. The anterior lobe consists of glandular tissue; it secretes six hormones and controls the release of others.

c. An intermediate lobe (not in humans) produces secretions that induce changes in fur color of vertebrates.

B. Posterior Lobe Secretions

1. The axons of neuron cell bodies in the hypothalamus extend down into the posterior lobe of the pituitary.

2. Two hormones are released into the capillary bed:

a. ADH acts on the walls of kidney tubules to control the body's water and solute levels.

b. Oxytocin triggers uterine muscle contractions to expel the fetus and acts on mammary glands to release milk.

C. Anterior Lobe Secretions

1. The anterior lobe releases six hormones that stimulate ("tropic") effects on other endocrine glands:

a. Corticotropin (ACTH) stimulates the adrenal cortex.

b. Thyrotropin (TSH) stimulates the thyroid gland.

c. Follicle&endash;stimulating hormone (FSH) stimulates egg formation in females and sperm formation in males.

d. Luteinizing hormone (LH) also acts on the ovary to release an egg and on the testes to release sperm.

e. Prolactin acts on the mammary glands to sustain milk production.

f. Somatotropin (STH) or growth hormone (GH) acts on body cells in general to promote growth.

2. The hypothalamus produces releasing and inhibiting hormones that target the anterior pituitary.

D. Examples of Abnormal Pituitary Output

1. The body does not produce large quantities of each hormone.

2. Experience has shown that the amounts, no matter how tiny, are critical to normal body functioning.

a. In childhood, too little GH can cause pituitary dwarfism, while too much causes gigantism.

b. Oversecretion of GH in adulthood causes a thickening of skin and bones called acromegaly.

III. The Thyroid and Parathyroid Glands

A. Thyroid Functioning

1. The human thyroid gland lies at the base of the neck in front of the trachea.

2. Two hormones, thyroxine and triiodothyronine, influence metabolic rates, growth, and development. Another, calcitonin, influences calcium levels in the blood.

a. The two hormones, thyroxine and triiodothyronine, contain critical amounts of iodine.

b. If the blood levels of iodine are too low, the pituitary responds with too much TSH, causing the thyroid gland to enlarge abnormally in what we call a goiter.

3. Hypothyroidism in adults results in lethargy and weight gain (decreased metabolic rate).

4. Hyperthyroidism increases heart rate and blood pressure and causes weight loss.

B. Parathyroid Functioning

1. These glands are embedded in the thyroid gland and respond to changing levels of calcium in the blood.

a. A drop in calcium level causes parathyroid hormone (PTH, or parathormone) levels to rise, resulting in removal of calcium from bone and an activation of vitamin D (to help in calcium absorption from the gut).

b. When calcium levels rise, the PTH levels are reduced.

2. Calcitonin from the thyroid gland acts antagonistically to PTH and promotes deposits of calcium in bones.

3. In response to low blood calcium, the parathyroids release PTH which causes releases of calcium from bones. If blood calciums become too high, the thyroid releases calcitonin which inhibits the release of calcium from bones.

IV. The Pancreas

A. The pancreas is a dual function gland; its exocrine function is to secrete digestive enzymes.

B. The endocrine portion consists of clusters of cells called the Islets of Langerhans, or islet cells. These islets within the pancreas are composed of three main cell types:

1. Alpha cells secrete glucagon, which causes glycogen stored in the liver to be converted to glucose, raising its levels in the blood.

2. Beta cells secrete insulin, which stimulates the uptake of glucose by the liver, muscle, and adipose cells to reduce glucose levels in the blood, especially after a meal.

3. Delta cells secrete somatostatin, which can inhibit the secretion of glucagon and insulin.

C. Diabetes mellitus is a disease resulting from imbalances of insulin; its effects include weight loss, ketone production, water&endash;solute problems, and possible death.

1. In type 1 diabetes, insulin is no longer produced because the beta cells have been destroyed by an autoimmune response; treatment is by insulin injection.

2. In type 2 diabetes, the insulin levels are near normal but the target cells cannot respond to the hormone; controlling diet is an effective treatment.

VI. The Sex Organs Secrete Steroid Hormones

1. The ovaries and testes, the gonads, produce gametes and hormones governing reproduction and development, the menstrual cycle and pregnancy via homeostatic feedback loops.

2. Sex hormones also influence secondary sexual traits.

3. The testes secrete several steroid hormones called androgens including testosterone.

4. The ovaries also secretes two types of steroid hormones, estrogen and progesterone.

5. The role of the sex hormones are discussed in Chapters 39 and 40. Good Luck.........

VII. Adrenal Gland

A. Adrenal Medulla

1. One adrenal gland is located on top of each kidney.

2. This inner portion secretes epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine (noradrenaline) under direction from sympathetic nerves from the hypothalamus.

3. Its secretions mobilize the body during times of excitement or stress ("fight&endash;or&endash;flight" response).

B. Adrenal Cortex

1. Among the secretions of the outer portion are the glucocorticoids such as cortisol, which helps control blood glucose levels (control blood glucose metabolism).

a. Cortisol secretion is an example of a homeostatic feedback loop.

b. When blood levels of glucose fall (as in hypoglycemia), the hypothalamus releases

CRH -> anterior pituitary -> ACTH -> adrenal cortex-> cortisol, which prevents muscle cells from withdrawing glucose from the blood.

c. When the body is stressed, as in painful injury, the nervous system provides an override mechanism in which the levels of cortisol remain high to promote healing.

2. The adrenal cortex also secretes aldosterone. When sodium levels falls, kidney cells secrete renin, which acts on the adrenal cortex to release aldosterone. Aldosterone promotes sodium reabsorption by the kidneys and sweat glands.

3. The adrenal cortex also produces the male sex hormone testosterone, albeit in smaller amounts than the sex organs produce. Tumors of the adrenal cortex sometimes leads to excessive testosterone release causing masculinization in women. i.e. facial hair on women, etc.

VIII. Pineal Gland

A. Located in the brain, this gland is sensitive to light and seasonal influences.

B. In the absence of light, melatonin is secreted; thus in winter, high levels of the hormone are instrumental in suppressing reproductive activity in hibernating animals.

C. Decreased melatonin secretion in humans might help trigger the onset of puberty. However, the function of the human pineal gland and melatonin is still unclear.

IX. Thymus

A. This gland is located behind the breastbone and between the lungs.

B. Certain lymphocytes multiply and mature in this gland; it also secretes groups of hormones called thymosins, which affect the functioning of lymphocytes that defend the body against disease (T cells).

X. The Kidney, Heart and Digestive Tract also produce Hormones........

A. Two hormones produced by the kidneys are important in regulating blood pressure and the blood oxygen carrying capacity; renin and erythropoietin.

1. When blood pressure falls, the kidneys release renin into the bloodstream. Renin acts as an enzyme catalyzing the formation of a second hormone angiotensin. Angiotensin in turn causes arterioles to constrict elevating blood pressure.

2. Constriction of the arterioles carrying blood to the kidneys also reduces the rate of blood filtration causing less water to be removed from the blood. Water retention causes an increase in blood volume and hence an increase in blood pressure.

3. In response to low blood oxygen levels, the kidneys release a second hormone erythropoietin. Erythropoietin travels in the blood to the bone marrow where it stimulates more rapid production of red blood cells whose role is to transport oxygen.

B. Atrial Natriuretic peptide (ANP), produced by cells in the atria of the heart, causes reduction of blood volume by decreasing the release of both ADH (water reabsorption; arterial constriction) and aldosterone (sodium reabsorption).

C. Four gastrointestinal hormones play roles in the digestive system.

1. Gastrin, produced by the stomach lining, stimulates secretion of acids into the stomach.

2. Secretin, from the intestinal lining, stimulates sodium bicarbonate secretions from the pancreas to neutralize the acidity of the incoming chyme.

3. Cholecystokinin enhances the actions of secretion and stimulates gallbladder contractions.

4. GIP (gastric inhibitory peptide), released in the presence of glucose and fat, inhibits acid production and peristalsis in the stomach.

D. Effects of Some Local Signaling Molecules

1. Prostaglandins are produced in tissues throughout the body; their actions remain localized near the site of secretion.

2. Epidermal growth factor (EGF) influences the growth of many cell types.

3. Nerve growth factor (NGF) promotes growth and survival of neurons in the developing embryo.

E. Comparative Look at a Few Invertebrate Hormones

1. Molting in arthropods is controlled by hormones.

2. Ecdysone promotes molting in insects; juvenile hormone retards the same process.
